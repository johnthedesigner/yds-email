Hello dahlia friends!

We are officially a club with members! And Spring is finally here! There is so much to celebrate that the past two weeks have been a whirlwind. Carol and I are on cloud 9 with excitement and that energy is keeping us fueled to keep checking off tasks on our club to-do list. In the meantime, we are looking forward to everyone getting to meet everyone else at our first Zoom meeting on Sunday, April 11th.

Yankee Dahlia Society is a club about dahlias, but also about our members. Most importantly, we are excited to connect, share and learn from each other. We have enjoyed getting to know you through posts on Instagram and Facebook and look forward to a fun year of speakers, tours and dahlia blooms.

If you know anyone that grows dahlias or may be interested in growing dahlias please tell them about Y.D.S.!

Y.D.S. is thrilled to have Paula Fisher from Garden Bee Flower Farm, located in the Piedmont Triad of North Carolina. Paula’s background includes volunteering as a master gardener since 2015. Her flower farming started six years ago with four 100-foot beds on her newly purchased property. After attending a flower farming workshop she jumped right into the role and since then her business has expanded to 10 times that original bed space.

Dahlias have taken the lead in what Paula really loves to grow and that has motivated her to grow over 200 varieties. The farm encompasses one acre of seasonal flowers and dahlias and produces 500-1000 dahlias weekly to a large floral industry wholesaler. She enjoys taking notes on the varieties she grows to evaluate which ones grow well in hot humid conditions.

Paula is also a member of the American Dahlia Society and her local chapter, Carolinas Dahlia Society in NC. She awaits the day for shows to resume and looks forward to showing dahlias. Paula will take us through her journey as a flower farmer, dahlia grower and enthusiast sharing a seasonal overview, some background behind the business and tips about growing dahlias from seed, cuttings and tubers. You don’t want to miss this opportunity to meet with Paula and join us for our first club meeting.

Dahlias 101

When to Plant Your Tubers

By Misty Florez

Many of us who have ordered tubers already have started to receive boxes in the mail. So the next decision to figure out is, When should you plant your tubers? You can take one of two paths; pre-sprouting your tubers by potting them up early indoors or waiting to plant tubers directly in the ground.

There are a few items to consider when making this decision. First, How many tubers do you have and will you have the space and conditions to keep them happy until they go outside? You don’t necessarily need a grow light to make pre-sprouting your tubers work, however the timing will then become very important. You’ll want to start tubers early enough so they get a jump on the growing season, but not so early that they become too leggy before you get the chance to plant in the garden. Starting around 4 weeks before you plant outdoors should give you an ideal head start!

Using grow lights is not necessary but will help in starting tubers indoors to prevent growth from stretching toward available window light. Place the grow lights directly above the plants leaving about 6” of space between the light and the plant, raising the lights as the plants grow. Remember not to water your tubers until you start to see signs of growth. Dahlia tubers are prone to rot caused by wet soil.

Whether you are pre-sprouting or planting directly into the garden you need to consider two more items and avoid planting too early. The green light to plant outdoors is when your soil temps have warmed and are consistently around 60 degrees. It doesn’t hurt to pick up a soil thermometer as they are relatively inexpensive. A seasonally late frost can damage tender plants, so it’s helpful to reference the Farmer’s Almanac and look up estimated last spring frost dates for your growing zip code. It will be safe to plant after your last possible frost and when the soil is warm. Again, even if you choose to directly plant your tubers outdoors, don't water until you start to see signs of growth. The tubers will be fine until then.
