Hello dahlia friends!

A busy spring is rolling into a busy summer. June included planting the last of our dahlias at our growing partner locations including roughly 150 tubers at Woburn Senior Center, 50 at Wright-Locke Farm, 89 at Peterfields Farm and delivering a few more tubers and cuttings to Grounded Flowers and Brilliant Dahlia Flower Farm. Our June club Zoom meeting was a great opportunity to share tactics and discuss how to manage the challenges of common Pests & Diseases in the garden. In the middle of the month, we had a wonderful opportunity to offer a Dahlia workshop for the public through our growing partner Wright-Locke Farm where we met a nice group of dahlia enthusiasts and shared How to Grow Dahlias: A Seasonal Overview. Meanwhile do you recall that over the winter Carol had been extremely successful for the very first time taking dahlia cuttings? With much delight we were able to put that effort towards helping build club stock and early in the month offer over 100 mature cuttings for sale! We are so glad many of you reached out and were able to take advantage of the cuttings sale and we were thrilled to have such a successful spring fundraiser. With a number of cuttings still available after the initial sale, we had a spontaneous idea to attend Winchester Farmers Market to offer them for sale as well as find an opportunity to network with our local community and the public. The weeks of June truly flew by as Carol and I enjoyed working together to promote the club with fresh ideas and trying new outlets to meet people interested in growing dahlias and reach out via avenues in the local communities. We have signed up a few new members along the way that we are beyond thrilled to have join the club.

In the meantime, it’s the middle of July already and we hope that everyone is having a wonderful summer enjoying time outdoors and in your gardens. The growing season has already experienced some extreme weather including 9 days of over 90 degree weather in June and 5 days in July. Add to that the heavy rain and strong winds tropical storm Elsa brought in the first week of the month, making it the wettest July in Boston in over 100 years.

We are very excited to hold our very first in person club meeting next Sunday, July 18th at one of our Growing Partner locations, Maplebrook Farmstead in Sterling, Massachusetts. Rain or shine we are ready for whatever unpredictable weather we might have and can gather under a very large tent. The day will feature a wonderful program with Joel and Vee, hands on demonstrations and a wonderful opportunity for everyone to meet each other and chat about the growing season. We will also take our very first club group photo. We welcome guests and friends that are interested in growing dahlias and learning more about Y.D.S. and we encourage you to please feel free to forward our email newsletter and extend the invitation, we simply ask that they RSVP.

– Misty & Carol

Video Tip

Using an Auger to help Staking Dahlias

The auger is a Ryobi cordless earth auger $249.99 from Home Depot. Coseytown originally shared a post about it on her IG page. Ours came with a 6” bit that works great for drilling holes for tubers (with no landscape fabric, or with fabric that has holes larger than 6” holes burned into it). The idea to pop a 2” bit on it to punch holes for stakes was a brilliant inspiration from above, lol!

The Ryobi tool head has a standard 7/8” drive shaft with a hole drilled through to accept a clevis pin which holds the bit on. Any non-Ryobi auger bit needs to be test fitted first to make sure it all lines up and fits.

I got the 2" bit from Harbor Freight. It is their Predator brand model. It was about $65. It fits the Ryobi shaft fine, but the tool head wobbles unless you add a small modification which I will explain. The hole for the clevis pin on the Predator bit is in the right place, but there is a very small space between the top of the bit and the top of the drive shaft that causes the annoying wobble and needs to be filled to make the bit assembly more rigid. So I first put a 7/8” split key ring (you could also find a metal washer the right thickness that has a 7/8” hole, but the split key ring was the only thing I had handy on the spot and it did the trick) on the drive shaft, then put the bit on. If you need pictures, let us know.

I also spray painted a red stripe around the bit at about 12” depth so I could see how far down to drill the holes. Works great. If you invent any awesome modifications, please share with us!

Growing Partners Introduction

Wright-Locke Farm (Winchester, MA)

Wright-Locke Farm is a community farm located in Winchester with a rich history dating back to the 17th century. The town of Winchseter purchased the 13 acres in 2007 and a Master Planning Committee and ultimately the Wright-Locke Farm Conservancy were created. The charm, history and the significance architecturally of the six structures include; the 1828 original Farmhouse, the 1827 Barn, the Squash House and the Ice house reflecting one of the few intact early nineteenth century farmsteads and New England farm life. The newest addition of the All-Seasons Barn was recently completed. It was a very large project for the farm that will be a wonderful expansion and important to the future success by and offer year round educational and event activities. The Farm is a special location creating a strong community through the variety of CSA programs, seasonal U-pick flowers and raspberries, educational classes and workshops for all ages. We are thrilled to be able to assist with growing and maintaining dahlias while offering educational workshops and connecting with the community through the farm.

The Farm is only a few miles away from the home of Misty Florez, club Co-President, making a partnership with a satellite dahlia garden for Y.D.S. a wonderful opportunity. The dahlia garden is next to the Farm’s beautiful cut flower field, an ideal location. With very little previous experience or background in growing dahlias Y.D.S. looks forward to sharing our knowledge and expertise growing and enjoying dahlias. The cut dahlias will be used in flower arrangements sold at the farm and the tuber clumps will be part of the club stock for future Y.D.S club stock and sales.

We are maximizing the number of dahlias in the dedicated area, however it was recognized that early on in the season the location was in need of some compost to grow in. Y.D.S. donated the resources and materials to improve the area for the dahlia garden. A few lumber boards, organic soil-cow compost mix sourced from Catlin Farmstead otherwise known as Otter River Black Gold and some elbow grease and in a few days 6 new raised beds were built. Later this month the addition of a new entry gate and simple fence to keep the bunnies and chickens at bay will complete the project.

Dahlia Tip

Pinching back, Disbranching and Disbudding

By Misty Florez & Carol Palmer

Pinching back, disbranching and disbudding: it’s all about plant maintenance, particularly if you are considering exhibiting your dahlia blooms at a local show. Words from experienced dahlia growers and winners will testify that plant maintenance is simply “good dahlia culture”. With a few key concepts you’ll be on your way to growing show quality dahlia blooms and get you ready to show dahlias.

Pinching back is also referred to as topping or stopping your dahlia plant. The practice involves removing the growing tip of the plant which then forces the plant to produce side branches for lateral growth. Ideally this is done when the dahlia plant is about 12” in height and has roughly 4 sets of leaves. Pinching the plant at this stage of growth will temporarily focus its energy towards producing leaves and stems rather than buds. It will take another 2 weeks for buds to appear but in the long run this technique creates bushier plants with more blooms. To do so find the uppermost sprout on the main stem and gently pull off the central growing tip with your fingers. Be careful not to damage the foliage around the tip.

Disbranching refers to the practice of removing lateral branches to limit the number of branches and blooms on a plant. It focuses the plant’s energy on those remaining blooms, making them larger and more vigorous. Serious growers focused on winning ribbons at shows consider this technique.
