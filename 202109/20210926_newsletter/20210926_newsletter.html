Hello dahlia friends!

September is almost over, and the days are getting cooler, drier and shorter. Our dahlias are really starting to come into their own, helping us forget about the rain and the wind of the months before and the approaching cold ahead. This was the month for dahlia shows. It was exciting to see many of you enjoy and participate in both national and local shows and congratulations to everyone that won awards. Well done!

The ADS National Show was hosted by the Dahlia Society of Ohio held in Wooster, Ohio (pronounced the same as our Worcester, Massachusetts) on September 9-12. Misty was able to go solo, taking two days to drive out to the Buckeye State and two days to drive back with a pit stop in each direction in New York. Clocking over 1400 miles in 6 days it was well worth the adventure. The dahlia blooms were absolutely stunning. It is amazing to see such perfection and care that is handled by each grower from all over the surrounding states. There were even some micros and single forms that were taken on a plane in a box from Portland, Oregon by Mark Oldenkamp. It was quite impressive to see first hand how it was done and watch these amazing growers exhibit so many beautiful blooms. This was the 2nd National show Misty was able to attend, but 1st time as a Co-Founder and Co-President of Y.D.S., one of the newest ADS chapter societies. It was wonderful to get to meet fellow Y.D.S. members: Paula Fisher from Garden Bee Flower Farm and her husband Ed, as well as Harry Rissetto. The experience was priceless and I am looking forward to staying connected to the many new dahlia friends around the country and hoping to see them next year in Reston, VA. Save-The-Dates for September 22-25, 2022 as the National Capital Dahlia Society will be the host.

We had a wonderful time for our September meeting hosted at Robin Sears’ beautiful home and garden in the historic district of South Hamilton. The weather was perfect, the garden was exploding with dahlias and the scones were absolutely delicious. Thank you Robin for taking us on a tour of your garden while sharing with us your favorite dahlia varieties. It was a delight to see Robin’s approach to growing dahlias throughout her garden rooms. Dahlias were incorporated into every corner of the landscape among other annuals, perennials and even vegetables. A gardener's delight. It is equally inspiring to watch Robin create a floral arrangement at ease and in her signature #kitchensinkbouquets style. We are so grateful to have Robin as a member of Y.D.S. Thank you again for hosting the club. For those that want to see more of Robin’s creativity check out her website Robin’s Nest Designs or follow her on Instagram @robinsears.

It’s not too early to start preparing for the time to dig your dahlia plants, even though they are in full bloom mode right now. See our Dahlia Tip section for some important reminders to take care of this month, before the frost hits and it’s too late. Preparation is key as always, but be sure to take time to just relax and enjoy being in your gardens too. Bloom time makes all the effort worthwhile.

– Misty & Carol

Dahlias from the Garden of YDS Member

Susan Bonnacci

Photography by: Susan Bonnacci

Pictured above 2nd Row (Left to Right): Seedling, Peaches and Cream, Myrtle's Folly and Star Child, Seedling

"I am a longtime backyard gardener with a relatively new passion for dahlias. We bought a fixer upper in downtown Beverly in 1992 and have been fixing up the house and the yard ever since. After considerable demolition and cleanup, I planted a series of borders with mostly traditional Spring perennials, until I finally realized my garden color and interest was basically over by late June. About 10 years ago, I bought a potted Babylon Purple dahlia in full bloom in a local garden shop (I still have it) and have been adding new dahlias every year since. Now I have dahlia gardens with a few Spring perennials mixed in.

This year I had about 400 dahlias (about 160 varieties) on our half acre plot and realize that is probably the max for me without ripping up the lawn. While that thought is always appealing in Jan/Feb I quickly realized, prior to every major storm (so quite often), that I already have as many dahlias as I can comfortably care for. So I basically have to Marie Kondo my 2022 garden. If it doesn’t bring me joy, it has to go to make room for any new additions. Imagine a dahlia not bringing you joy?!?!?!?

My first love was pink/blush dahlias with Tahoma Stellar Feller, Castle Drive/Coralie and Lakeview Premier being my favorites. This year I have also fallen for the rusty dark pinks like Snoho Jo Jo, Ferncliff Rusty and Mission Gypsy. Top of my 2022 list is Nuland’s Josephine and Polventon Kristobel, both UK varieties that may be nearly impossible to get. A girl can dream!

My biggest challenge is winter storage with a garage that is too cold and a basement that is too warm. Last year we fashioned an insulated corner of the basement with a fan to bring in cold outside air and still couldn’t reliably keep temperatures below 50 degrees. As a result, I would check every tuber box about every 3 weeks and rehydrate the peat moss when necessary. What a mess! On the upside, it gave me something to do during those long, cold months. When I get sick of doing that, I begin to pot up tubers in the house - I’m embarrassed to say that I do that in early March. I move them to the garage as soon as it is warm enough and move them into and out of the sun daily. My family and my neighbors think I’m crazy. Maybe so, but I had blooms in June."

Dahlia Tip

Inspect and label your dahlia plants now before it’s too late

September is a good time to go carefully through your dahlia garden, checking each plant. There are at least two reasons for doing this. First is to identify any plants that may appear diseased or possibly have a virus. If you have a plant you think may be diseased but you aren’t sure, post a picture to our Community page on Facebook. It’s important to know that testing is the only way to conclusively confirm if a plant is virused, but posting photos helps all of us together learn what to look for in our dahlias and to grow clean disease-free plants that will thrive in our gardens.

Second, you want to confirm that each variety is correctly identified. If you aren’t sure what the bloom of each variety should look like, the ADS website Online Classification Guide has lots of photos to help you make a positive identification. It’s a good growing practice to keep the variety name with the tuber at each seasonal step. Before frost hits, write the variety name of each plant that you have positively identified on a length of high visibility plastic flagging ribbon tied around the bottom of the stem. This will ensure that the variety name will stay with the plant once you’ve cut down all your plants in preparation for digging the tubers.

We sell the very excellent Garden Markers for this purpose. These magnificent markers do not fade or wear off like standard markers do (don’t be fooled, those Sharpies will fade)! The writing remains as dark and legible as the day you wrote it for well more than an entire season. We won’t consider using anything else to write on our labels, and neither should you. We will have these markers available for purchase at our October 3rd meeting. $3 each, or 3 for $8. We will also have white plant tags to tie around the base of the main stem; 20 white plant tags for $2.00.
