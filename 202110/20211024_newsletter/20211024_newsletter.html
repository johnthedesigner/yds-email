Hello dahlia friends!

Almost the end of October already and we still haven’t had a killing frost yet. Cooler temps should be upon us this week with lots of rain. We have certainly enjoyed the dahlias blooming in our gardens and seeing lots of your beautiful blooms and photos on social media.

Over the years we have learned that the more dahlia plants you grow means the more you have to manage, dig up and divide. The bonus is that growing lots of tubers for the club will be a wonderful source of tuber stock to offer for our club tuber sale. The past three weeks have kept us busy sticking to our schedule of cutting dahlia plants back and we are halfway through lifting them out of the ground. It’s hard to do especially when the weather is still so mild and there are still dahlias blooming.

We appreciate the generosity of all our 7 Growing Partners for hosting and caring for our plants over the past 5.5 months and working with the schedule to tag, cut and dig up plants. We couldn’t have done it without you!

The next two months are going to be the big push to get all these tuber clumps divided and inventoried for our sale in January. It is the perfect opportunity for new growers to work with experienced growers to learn how to divide and store tubers for the winter. Instead of having our typical club meeting, we decided to have 9 weeks of opportunity for all of you to learn and participate in dividing club tubers and getting to know each other as we prepare for the first club tuber sale. We are so excited for the response we have received with all those who have jumped in and signed up already to help with these tasks. It’s a wonderful way to get to know each other as well as contribute your valuable time to the club’s success. For those that have signed up, Thank you! Please bring with you dividing supplies: a pair of clippers and pruners. Feel free to bring your lunch, but we will have light refreshments during our dividing days.

Check out the calendar on SignUp Genius as we set aside lots of dates and times hoping there is some time that works for you to help out. Please RSVP through SignUp Genius. It will help us anticipate who is coming and manage the workdays while sending you reminders of your signups and allowing you to change your signups if something comes up in your schedule.

Dahlias from the Garden of YDS Member

Lanette Lepper of Armstrong Acres

Photography by: Lanette Lepper

"My name is Lanette Lepper and I am a new member of the YDS. We recently settled ‘back home’ in Dudley, MA after my husband Stephen retired from 21 years of service in the Navy. We are continuing a long tradition of family agriculture in the area. My great-great grandfather, Chester Armstrong, was an Overseer at the Dudley Town Farm. My great-grandfather, Walter Armstrong, was Warden at the Webster Poor Farm, and my grandparents, Bob and Sigrid Armstrong, purchased the property we are currently tending almost 100 years ago. Our farm, Armstrong Acres, is named in their honor.

We are proud to be the first Homegrown by Heroes certified farm in the state. Homegrown by Heroes is the official farmer veteran branding program of America, administered nationally by the Farmer Veteran Coalition. It certifies farmers, ranchers, and fishermen of all military eras to sell their products as veteran-owned and produced. The label informs consumers that agricultural products displaying the logo were produced by U.S. military veterans, and allows veterans to differentiate their farm and ranch products in the marketplace. It provides consumers with a tangible way to support veterans.

Although the farm is 28 acres, our growing space is only about 30 x 100’ and expanding slowly. We inherited the garden from my Uncle Leroy, who had been growing there since the 60s. As he got older, it decreased in size but we’re in the process of enlarging it again. In addition to cut flowers for the bouquet-subscription delivery service we offer, I grow lots of the usual veggies. I love heirloom tomatoes (The seedling sale we hosted this spring boasted more than 40 different varieties of hard-to-find heirlooms!) and I enjoy preserving the harvest by canning, dehydrating, and freezing.

I am passionate about many things... math (high school teacher by trade, and I still sub regularly), cross-stitch, reading, and of course gardening. I love that every season in the garden is different and there are always, always new lessons to be learned! I also enjoy sharing that passion with others and in the past have blogged for Mother Earth News, taught classes in person and online, and have done presentations for garden clubs and libraries. I was a beekeeper until a bear found my hives last fall. (That hobby has been put on hold until we are able to build Ft. Knox Apiary!) In the future, I’m hoping to host classes and tomato tastings on the farm.

This was actually my first year growing Dahlias! My grandmother grew them, so it was always in the back of my mind to return them to the property. However, I was hesitant because they are so high maintenance, and I wasn’t looking forward to digging them every year. But once I started following other flower farmers on Instagram, I could no longer resist the call! I grew about 18 plants... 9 different varieties from various sources (it would have been more, but by the time I decided to grow them, most places were sold out.) My two favorites that I grew this year are the Arabian Nights (early and prolific bloomer with great vase life) and the Jowey Lindas, which have totally changed my mind about orange flowers! Varieties I have fallen in love with and hope to add to my collection include Peaches and Cream, Thomas Edison, and Cornel Bronze. I’m also hoping to grow Nettie, because that’s a nickname that only my closest friends are allowed to call me! :-) My favorite forms are Decorative and Ball, and while I don’t have a favorite color, I sure am looking forward to adding purples and pinks to my collection! My biggest challenge growing them is definitely earwigs, and I need to amp up my staking game next season. I am really wanting to learn more about which varieties have the best vase-life so that I can continue to wow my customers with how long locally grown flowers will last! Those who would like to learn more can follow our journey on Instagram @ArmstrongAcresMA."

Indelible Pencils

As you begin to acquire more dahlia plants in your garden, keeping the variety names with the plants becomes a very valuable asset as both your knowledge and dahlia collection grows.

Indelible Pencils are very helpful to label your tubers individually. Used on a moist tuber, the pencil leaves a highly legible and very long lasting mark, making it possible to still read your tubers even after a full season in the ground. The Indelible Pencils are easy to use and extremely superior to a Sharpie marker, and once you try them you’ll never want to use anything else.

Indelible Pencils $3.00/each or 3 for $8.00

Flart Snips

We also have Flart Floral Snips available for sale. We initially discovered them on a post by @Santacruzdahlias recommending them. We tried them out ourselves and found them to be fantastic as they are perfect for both cutting blooms and for dividing. One snip and you will be in love. They cut like a dream, have a comfortable grip, non-stick high carbon steel blades for a smooth cut, and a pointy tip that really can get in tight areas when dividing tuber clumps.

Flart Floral Snips $25.00

Vermiculite

There are many ways to store your dahlia tubers for the winter. One method for overwintering your tubers is to store them in a “medium” of coarse vermiculite in a small shoe box-like plastic container.

We are offering bags of coarse vermiculite for sale. Each bag contains 24 quarts of vermiculite, more than enough to completely fill four of the 6 Quart Sterilite Storage Containers from Walmart . Key to note is that you won’t “fill” each container with vermiculite when storing dahlias. We estimate that one bag of vermiculite will fill roughly 8 containers with vermiculite and dahlias.

Pick up only option on the vermiculite. Pick up outside the scheduled dividing workdays can be arranged.

Coarse Vermiculite, 24 quart bag $16.99

Digging Up Dahlias

In our growing climate because our ground freezes in the winter, dahlias are considered tender perennials. This requires digging up the tuber clump at the end of the growing season to overwinter your tubers indoors. So one of the two end of the dahlia season growing tasks is to lift tubers out of the ground. The short handle garden fork is our favorite to speed and ease the process. We purchased our Garden Fork at Harbor Freight Tools for $21.00. It is important to remember when using a garden fork to work your fork at a radius of at least 12” from the center stalk coming out of the ground. This will keep your tool at a safe distance from puncturing the tubers. Keep working around the tuber clump lifting gently to break off the feeder roots, eventually freeing the clump from the ground. Gently lift the clump by the “stem handle” as well as put your hand or fork under the clump to keep the weight off the stem, as the stem might not be secure to the clump.
