Hello dahlia friends!

June has been an exciting and busy month for us all, with planting lots of dahlias in our gardens, and at our eight YDS Growing Partner locations. We have planted many beautiful and new to YDS varieties at our Growing Partner locations this year and have had a great time doing it thanks to the support of all our wonderful members who volunteered their time to help out and to our Growing Partners for sharing their farms and garden space. We could not have done it without all of you! We are really looking forward to bringing all of you a great selection of exciting varieties for our next tuber sale later this year.

Dahlia garden at Woburn Senior Center
Photos by: Misty Florez

We just had a fantastic Zoom meeting in June. Our special guests were dahlia virus expert Dr. Hanu Pappu of Washington State University, and Ron Miner, Chair of the ADS Virus Research Team. Dr. Pappu shared a wealth of information with us about dahlia virus and the ways viruses can be transmitted from plant to plant, showed us photos of some of the most common symptoms of virus and talked about the exciting findings that virus free dahlias can be successfully propagated by tissue culture. Ron Miner showed us how and where to find all the information available about dahlia virus and testing on the ADS website, and explained the ADS Clean Stock Initiative, a program which includes subsidized testing of dahlias for virus for local clubs and individuals through Dr. Pappu’s lab. Ron also recommended the ADS good practices for keeping virus out of our gardens: disinfecting cutting tools between plants, and disposing of plants that may be suspected of having a virus. The goal of the ADS Clean Stock Initiative is to have and maintain healthy virus free dahlia plants in our gardens. YDS was pleased to make a donation of $200 to the ADS Virus Research Project. We are glad to support the work of Dr. Pappu and his research lab at Washington State University, and the ADS Virus Research Team. Concluding the program YDS raffled off a pair of Flart Silky Teflon Floral Snips to a lucky member who attended the meeting, as part of the effort to promote the practice of disinfecting our tools between plants. Amy Liss was the lucky winner of the Flart Floral Snips. Nice score, Amy!

We recorded the meeting and presentations for those of you who weren't able to attend or who want to see it again, and we recommend it. So much good information was shared. The recording is now available in our 2022 past Zoom recordings folder.*

Back in early May with the help of several YDS members we potted up a few hundred tubers to grow into rooted plants in anticipation of having a plant sale. In the middle of June we held a sale of rooted dahlia plants at the Natick Farmers Market. The weather was beautiful, it was a lot of fun, and we met lots of people, many of them brand new dahlia growers who were excited to grow their first dahlia plants ever. Several of them became new members of YDS. We welcome them to the club, and are looking forward to seeing lots of photos of first time blooms from everyone’s new plants. We are sure to see some awesome blooms and can’t wait to share them on social media. We are looking forward to doing it again next year.

We are thrilled to be growing approximately 200 dahlia plants this year at Nashoba Valley Winery & Bolton Beer Works in Bolton MA. This beautiful winery and restaurant in central Massachusetts is well worth a visit, and not simply for the dahlias there. Read more about our newest Growing Partner below.

Our next meeting will be our first garden tour of the year; we will be bringing you something new and a little different, and it’s sure to be lots of fun. We will enjoy a combined dahlia and lily garden tour at the home of YDS member Roy Christoph in Wenham MA. Roy grows nearly 300 dahlias and over 1500 lily plants, which will be in full bloom for us to admire and enjoy. Bring your cameras, as this stunning display is not to be missed.

– Carol & Misty

Member Spotlight

Jessica Wilke

Photos by: Jessica Wilke

flowersat60worcester

Flowers at 60 Worcester

Greetings Dahlia friends! My name is Jess and I garden on a steep third of an acre in West Boylston, MA. I also keep a roadside stand, Flowers at 60 Worcester, specializing in local, florist-quality blooms such as dahlias and fancy narcissus. I’ve been growing on this property for 4 years, however the history of my garden appears to go back to 1774- the date inscribed on a board in the attic of this antique home. I often wonder who planted the floods of old fashioned bluebells, foxglove and lily of the valley that spread across the garden beds year after year. The house was the first general store in West Boylston where you just might have been able to buy seeds and imported bulbs and according to the historical record, massive quantities of local rum! It’s apparent that gardening has been important to the many keepers of this house and I am glad to be the newest contributor to this special place.

We have very little lawn and mostly garden beds as the property has been terraced into the hill with sunny top levels that descend down into shade with a small brook bubbling at the bottom. I grow dahlias and other cut flowers on the top levels near the house and have a lot of work to do in the future to get the lower levels into a more maintenance-free area of perennials and ground cover. I do have a few other plant interests such as hosta, hellebore, spring ephemerals and epimedium that followed me from a previous shady home. Gardening is my release from a hectic life as a mom and music teacher as well as part-time florist and flower grower. I love the quietness and peace of working in the garden as it is the complete opposite of my work as an elementary school music teacher. I also enjoy the physical aspect of getting my hands dirty with hard work. My least favorite part of gardening, especially on this property, is managing slugs. Perhaps this house was a slug farm at some point in history… the problem is epic and I battle them daily during the growing season.

I began growing dahlias 4 years ago but have had a lifelong love of gardening as have my parents, grandparents and great grandparents. I was so excited to have sun to work with when we moved here from a bungalow tucked beneath giant shade trees. I went to graduate school in the Bay Area where the dahlias were stunning and I wanted to give them a try. I started with 10 and as the story often goes it became 50, then 200, and this year I am looking at about 400 in total as I am also keeping a small plot at my son’s horseback riding stable. I love ball dahlias but also have a soft spot for wackier informal decoratives. I tend to lean towards orange/ peach dahlias but also love dark red/blacks and purples. I attempt to keep a lot of colors and look for varieties with a good vase life to support the needs of the flower stand.

For me, the challenge of growing dahlias is a big part of the thrill. From storing, dividing, sprouting and pinching to supporting the plants there is just so much to learn. The reward is great when you have a spectacular armload to share! Like most people new to dahlias there was a steep learning curve to the process. I lost all but a variety or two in storage my first year and storage continues to be my greatest challenge as my basement stays surprisingly warm. My tubers get stashed in unheated rooms in our upstairs inside of heavy duty styrofoam coolers filled with vermiculite. I think cracking the mystery of storage in our cold, dry climate is our very biggest challenge locally. Should I manage to grow all 400 dahlias this year, I have no clue how I will manage to store them all!

I think my best advice to those just starting out with dahlias is to read, watch and listen to everything you can get your hands on and ask questions about the entire process when in need of help. Don’t be afraid to mess things up as the plants truly want to grow- sometimes it’s easy to forget that there is always next year. Social media is an amazingly rich source of learning material and with a few clicks you can be in touch with top dahlia experts, the best growers and breeders as well as the wealth of knowledge within our own club! Almost everything I have learned about these amazing flowers has come from those willing to share their knowledge on these platforms- I really think this is the most valuable aspect of the greater dahlia community!

Wishing you a successful growing season with lots of blooms and no signs of those pesky slugs. Please feel free to reach out at any time, stop by the flower stand or walk the sidewalks in front of my biggest growing plot.

Dahlia Tip

Battling Beetles

By Carol Palmer

Left: Japanese beetle, Middle: Asian Beetles, Right: Oriental Beetles
Photos by Chuck Bargeron, University of Georgia, Bugwood.org and Misty Florez

June is the month when we generally begin seeing beetles in our gardens. These insect pests overwinter in the soil as grubs, and begin to emerge as adult beetles in early summer, tapering off in early fall. Beetles feed on leaves and flowers, including dahlias, leaving holes in foliage and turning flower petals brown and wilted. Here are some tips you can try to help control or eliminate them:

Long Term Management:

Treat your lawn with milky spore bacteria. This bacteria is harmless to humans and animals, but will cause death to grubs that ingest it as they feed on treated grass roots.

Immediate Management:

Hand Pick Beetles off Dahlias
Photo by John Livornese

Beetles can be hand picked off plants and dropped into a bowl of soapy water. The soap keeps them from being able to fly out of the water. Use a headlamp or a flashlight to hand pick beetles off plants at night.

Bag Trap
Photo by Misty Florez

Beetle bag traps are readily available and you may be considering adding one to your garden. They contain a scent lure that attracts beetles into a plastic bag. Some gardeners believe the traps work well and are very effective at reducing the beetle population in their garden. Others believe the traps merely serve to attract large numbers of beetles to their garden, making the problem worse. You can read more about it in the link below to an interesting article from The University of Minnesota Extension. If you decide to try out a bag trap, let us know what you think!

University of Minnesota Extension (June 27, 2022)

Captain Jack’s Dead Bug
Photo by Misty Florez

Spray plants with an insecticide product (ideally one approved for organic gardening) such as Captain Jack’s Dead Bug Brew

Cover Bloom with Organza Bags
Photo by Misty Florez

Tie organza bags over the blooms to create a bugproof barrier.

Find more tips on organic control of Japanese beetles on dahlias in an article written by Tony Evangelista in the June 2022 issue of the ADS Bulletin (pp. 67-69)

Update: Dahlia Cuttings Medium

By Andrea Campbell

Growing Mediums & Dahlia Cuttings; Photo by Andrea Campbell

Our March Meeting was all about starting cuttings and pot tubers. At the meeting Carol and Misty discussed different types of rooting mediums dahlia growers may use to start their cuttings. Every member in attendance was sent home with some sample mediums to try out.

Andrea Campbell went home and tested the different types of mediums and here is what she shared and the results.

“I had a few tubers to plant that had long sprouts. I thought it would be best to remove the sprouts and instead of tossing them, I potted them. I used all of the mediums you provided to us during your cuttings presentation. I took these cuttings in the dahlia garden as I was planting...super informal although I did sterilize the scalpel after each use. After two weeks they all rooted. I would say the one in the dark green medium, Root Riot Cubes did the best, then the ones in the sand and last the one in the stonewool. They all look healthy and well and are now potted up in soil.”

American Dahlia Society: New Judging Handbook

Photos by Misty Florez

By Carol Palmer

ADS has just published the new, updated Guide to Judging Dahlias. The new updated Guide has been extensively reworked, with many helpful color photographs now added.

For the past two years, meeting every four to six weeks via Zoom, the ADS Judging Guideline Committee has been working together to complete the new guide. First published in 1953, there have been several revisions over the years as judges have changed their methods and ideas about judging dahlias, with the last revision being from 2013. This new guide reflects the current ADS standards for judging dahlias, and is a valuable resource not only for those interested in showing and judging dahlias, but also for any dahlia grower who wants to learn more about the dahlias in their garden. Every ADS member this year will get a copy of the new updated Guide to Judging Dahlias.

Not yet an ADS member? Individual membership is available for $24 annually, or a Family membership (two people at the same address) for $27. Email us if you’d like to become a member of ADS and we will take care of the details. Subject Line: I'd like to join ADS!

Our August meeting will be via Zoom, and will feature a presentation with special guests from Mid Island Dahlia Society, Jim Teeple and Kathy Burke, on what judging dahlias is all about, and what it is like to become an ADS judge. Would you like to know more about all the many dahlia forms, and what makes a good dahlia bloom? Are you planning to show your dahlias in September, either locally or at the national show? Would you just like to have a better idea about what are the different characteristics of all the dahlias you grow in your garden? The new updated Guide to Judging Dahlias is a must have resource to expand your dahlia knowledge. Join ADS today and receive a free copy and all the benefits of an annual ADS membership! 2022 Growing Partner Introduction

Nashoba Valley Winery & Bolton Beer Works

Photo by Misty Florez

We would like to introduce to you our newest Growing Partner, Nashoba Valley Winery & Bolton Beer Works in Bolton MA. Nashoba Valley Winery has a beautiful location set amidst grape vines, fruit trees and hills, with lovely display beds at the front of the winery and restaurant. The winery is well known for their fruit wines made on the premises, as well as a brewery and a restaurant. YDS has planted dahlias in traditional staked rows with drip irrigation in a large former vegetable garden, and there are also many more dahlias tucked into several stone lined beds in a more freeform style in the display garden at the front of the property. The beds will be beautiful when the dahlias begin to bloom. The winery grounds are beautiful in an extensive orchard setting, and it is well worth a visit. We are excited to be partnering with Nashoba Valley Winery this year. Thank you YDS member Gayle Joseph for your help at the Winery!
